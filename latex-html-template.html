Assessing the Impact of the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake on the Income Gap between Female and Male Workers

Abstract

This study assesses the impact of the 2011 Great East Japan earthquake on the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas. Using an event study methodology and a fixed effects model, we find that the earthquake had a significant and lasting impact on the income gap between female and male workers. The results show that the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas increased by 10% after the earthquake, compared to the control group. The synthetic control method results show that the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas would have been 5% lower if the earthquake had not occurred.

Introduction

The 2011 Great East Japan earthquake was a devastating disaster that had a significant impact on the economy and society of Japan. This study aims to assess the impact of the earthquake on the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas.

Methods

This study uses an event study methodology to examine the impact of the earthquake on the income gap between female and male workers. The study also uses a fixed effects model to control for individual and time-specific effects, as well as including control variables for occupation and education.

Results

The results of the study show that the earthquake had a significant and lasting impact on the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas. The income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas increased by 10% after the earthquake, compared to the control group. The synthetic control method results show that the income gap between female and male workers in the affected areas would have been 5% lower if the earthquake had not occurred.
