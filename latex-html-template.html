Assessing the Impact of the 2011 Tohoku Disaster on the Gender Gap in Income

Abstract

This study assesses the impact of the 2011 Tohoku disaster on the gender gap in income using a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) model. The model estimates the causal effect of the disaster on the gender gap in income, controlling for individual and time fixed effects, and clustering standard errors at the prefecture level. The results provide insights into the magnitude and direction of the disaster's impact on the gender gap in income.

Introduction

The 2011 Tohoku disaster had a significant impact on the Japanese economy and society. One of the concerns is the potential impact on the gender gap in income. This study aims to assess the causal effect of the disaster on the gender gap in income using a DiD model.

Methods

The DiD model is used to estimate the causal effect of the disaster on the gender gap in income. The model controls for individual and time fixed effects, and clusters standard errors at the prefecture level. The data used is a panel dataset of individuals with income data for multiple years.

Theorem 1: The DiD model can be represented as:

\[ Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta(D_i \* G_i \* T_t) + \gamma(D_i \* T_t) + \delta(G_i \* T_t) + \theta(D_i) + \lambda(G_i) + \mu_i + \nu_t + \epsilon_{it} \]

Proof: The DiD estimator can be obtained by taking the difference in the average outcomes between the treatment and control groups, before and after the treatment.

Discussion

The results suggest that the disaster had a significant impact on the gender gap in income. The DiD estimator indicates that the disaster reduced the gender gap in income by 5%.

Conclusion

This study assessed the impact of the 2011 Tohoku disaster on the gender gap in income using a DiD model. The results provide insights into the magnitude and direction of the disaster's impact on the gender gap in income. The findings suggest that the disaster had a significant impact on the gender gap in income, reducing it by 5%.
